# Species And Entry Compatibility

Status: candidate canon
Provenance: generated from creator direction.

## Core Principle

The System contains or connects worlds with every conceivable and inconceivable form of life somewhere in the multiverse.

That does not mean every kind of life appears in every world.

Like attracts like. Scenario instances cluster entrants, species, cultures, memory settings, body plans, senses, and reality assumptions by compatibility. This is not absolute segregation, but it makes shared worlds more coherent and survivable.

## Compatibility Clustering

World instances may cluster by:

- body plan;
- sensory model;
- environment;
- social cognition;
- lifespan;
- communication mode;
- memory retention rules;
- metaphysics;
- violence tolerance;
- technology/magic expectations;
- narrative or "play style";
- entry requirements;
- cultural acclimation burden;
- viewer/sponsor/god-layer needs;
- psychological survivability.

Some lifeforms are so different from humans that they would rarely share a scenario with humanoids. If they did, humans might not recognize them as people, participants, ecosystems, gods, weather, architecture, dreams, or background processes.

## Humanoid-Weighted World

The target world can have many peoples while remaining generally humanoid-compatible.

Reason:

The MC's selected instance is likely clustered around forms, cultures, and cognition patterns that a modern Earthborn human can adapt to. It may still contain nonhuman, strange, or rare peoples, but the dominant participant-compatible band is humanoid or humanoid-legible.

This supports abundance without turning the starting region into incoherent species soup.

## Race / Species Selection

Entrants may be able to select species, ancestry, lineage, body plan, or compatible local people during entry configuration.

The System may not present this as a simple "race select" screen by default. It may frame it as:

- incarnation compatibility;
- body plan;
- lineage;
- ancestry;
- local integration path;
- species vector;
- culture package;
- birth family compatibility;
- soul/body fit.

## Cross-Species Entry Advice

If an entrant selects a species or people very different from their prior incarnation, the System strongly recommends a from-birth or early-life path.

Reason:

Birth-path entry gives time to learn:

- body instincts;
- senses;
- movement;
- language;
- culture;
- family norms;
- taboo structure;
- social signals;
- diet;
- emotion expression;
- danger cues;
- magic interface;
- species-specific cognition;
- local childhood training.

Adult arrival into an unfamiliar species is possible, but risky.

## Cultural Ineptitude

Some entrants ignore the System's advice.

This creates story pressure:

- adult arrivals who chose a new species but cannot read body language;
- people who offend their own supposed culture immediately;
- entrants who misunderstand courtship, hierarchy, scent, gaze, touch, territory, food, modesty, gender, caste, kinship, or violence norms;
- adventurers who selected powerful bodies but lack the instincts to use them safely;
- social disasters caused by assuming "humanoid" means "human inside";
- locals who treat these entrants as cursed, possessed, disabled, foreign, rude, or dangerous.

This also gives the MC a comparative advantage if he chooses newborn entry. He pays the cost of helplessness but receives deep cultural and bodily acclimation.

## Representation Density

Species can appear at different densities:

- dominant regional peoples;
- common minorities;
- trade-route minorities;
- diaspora groups;
- isolated enclaves;
- rare visitors;
- unique entrants;
- hidden/non-recognized participants;
- nonlocal entities only seen through avatars;
- lifeforms present but not recognized as people.

Sparse representation can create strong character hooks. A unique or near-unique entrant may have no local cultural support, no compatible teachers, and no one who understands their body or magic.

## Play Style And Entry Requirements

Worlds may have "play style" tendencies without being literal games.

Candidate dimensions:

- adventure/combat;
- settlement/crafting;
- court/politics;
- divine story arcs;
- exploration;
- survival;
- scholarly/magic research;
- dungeon/ecosystem management;
- trade/economy;
- romance/social bond;
- war/strategy;
- spiritual cultivation;
- restoration/recovery.

Species and entry options may be gated by compatibility with these tendencies.

Examples:

- a hive-mind species may require group-entry infrastructure;
- an aquatic species may require water-rich regions or vessel support;
- a long-lived species may require different memory-retention settings;
- a species with nonvisual senses may need a different System interface;
- a nonindividual lifeform may not map cleanly to classes or titles;
- a highly alien cognition may not share the same death-interface language.

## Starting Region Recommendation

For the MC's initial story, use a humanoid-compatible region with a limited exposed roster.

Recommended initial mix:

- humans or near-humans as common baseline;
- two to four common humanoid peoples with clear cultural roles;
- one or two visibly nonhuman but socially integrated peoples;
- rumors of stranger entrants/peoples elsewhere;
- at least one culturally inept cross-species entrant as a cautionary or comic/dramatic example.

This lets the world feel broad without overwhelming the newborn/childhood phase.

## Population Scope Progression

Use an MMORPG-style scope curve for exposed peoples.

Early areas can be racially or culturally limited, even insular, so the reader and MC learn a coherent local baseline first. This does not mean the world is isolated. It means the starting lens is local.

As the MC moves outward, the population mix becomes more complex:

1. **Household / Village / Starter Locality**
   Mostly one dominant people or culture, with a few known outsiders.

2. **Town / Market / Road Network**
   Typically one to three mingling peoples or race groupings are resident or routinely present. Nearby peoples appear through trade, marriage, labor, temples, soldiery, festivals, and peddlers. Less common peoples are usually travelers, specialists, or events rather than part of the everyday baseline.

3. **City / Guild Hub / Regional Capital**
   Multiple peoples are socially visible, with districts, guilds, embassies, minority communities, and mixed institutions.

4. **Frontier / Adventuring / War Zones**
   Mercenaries, soldiers, dungeon crews, refugees, pilgrims, specialists, and rare entrants appear.

5. **Continental / Imperial / Planar / System-Linked Regions**
   Species complexity becomes normal, and local assumptions break down.

Traveling groups provide early glimpses:

- peddlers;
- merchants;
- bards;
- soldiers;
- mercenaries;
- pilgrims;
- monster hunters;
- guild factors;
- diplomats;
- refugees;
- caravan guards;
- scholars;
- healers;
- tinkers;
- priests;
- adventuring parties.

This gives the MC an insular childhood without making the world feel small. Outsiders become events, rumors, stories, warnings, temptations, and early data points for the Inner Workshop.

Candidate starting-region rosters are drafted in `canon/realms/starting-region-roster-options.md`.

Typical local population guidance:

- village: usually one dominant people, with rare outsiders;
- town: usually one to three resident/mingling peoples or race groupings;
- market hub: three to five familiar peoples, plus regular travelers;
- city: many peoples, but still structured by districts, guilds, class, and culture;
- frontier/adventuring hub: unusually diverse, because danger and opportunity pull specialists together.

## Provocateur Pass

- Assumptions to confirm:
  - Entrants can select species/body/lineage under some conditions.
  - Cross-species selection is possible but strongly recommends birth-path acclimation.
  - The target world is humanoid-compatible by instance clustering.
- Possible conflicts:
  - Too much species choice can make local culture feel arbitrary.
  - Too little species diversity underuses the multiverse premise.
  - "Race select" terminology may feel too gamey unless framed through incarnation compatibility.
- Missing decisions:
  - What are the starting region's common peoples?
  - Are species choices constrained by prior body, soul shape, scenario rules, or cost?
  - Can someone choose a powerful species and bypass social/physical training?
  - How does memory retention interact with nonhuman childhood?
- Drift risks:
  - Generic fantasy race list.
  - Treating species as stat packages only.
  - Ignoring culture, senses, bodies, diet, taboos, family structure, and social signals.
- Useful pressure tests:
  - What mistake does a culturally inept adult-arrival entrant make on day one?
  - What species would look attractive in the menu but be miserable to inhabit?
  - What local people are humanoid-legible but not human-minded?
  - What lifeform might be present in the starting region that humans do not recognize as a participant?
  - Which traveling outsider first makes the MC realize the starting region is only a tiny compatibility pocket?
