# MC Household Options

Status: candidate options
Provenance: generated from starting-region skeleton and creator workflow direction.

These options place the MC inside the Hearthroad Vale. They are not approved canon yet.

## Design Goals

The household should support:

- newborn-entry costs;
- privacy loss and dependency;
- early System-interface research;
- practical exposure to craft, language, trade, and local magic;
- enough outsiders to feed the Inner Workshop without making childhood cosmopolitan;
- social pressure if the MC is strange;
- future hooks into class, profession, guild, and travel systems.

## Option 1: Road-Inn Brewery Household

Working household name:

**The Glen Door**

Location:

Briar Glen, on the local road spur between farms and Threefold Crossing.

Family role:

The family runs a modest inn, stable-yard, brew shed, and provisioning kitchen. It is not a grand traveler's inn; it is the place locals use for meetings, cart repairs, small ale, guest-right, news, and road meals.

Household economy:

- ale and small beer;
- mead in good years;
- bread, stew, and preserved food;
- stable stalls;
- cart storage;
- traveler bedding;
- message holding;
- simple trade brokerage;
- honey, wax, and herbs from nearby suppliers.

Parents/caretakers:

- **Mother candidate:** Hearthfolk innkeeper/brewer's daughter or widow's daughter who married into the household.
- **Father candidate:** practical host, stable manager, brewer, or road provisioner.
- **Ashenkin tie:** forge/kiln family repairs kettles, hoops, hinges, ovens, and chimney stone; one Ashenkin aunt/neighbor/foster relation visits often.
- **Caregiver/nurse candidate:** hired or kin-linked young woman from a poorer Hearthfolk or Ashenkin-adjacent household, creating later social and emotional complications.

Early data streams:

- overheard traveler speech;
- bargaining;
- songs;
- rumors;
- road warnings;
- recipes;
- brewing schedules;
- yeast/fermentation observations;
- stable animal behavior;
- guest-right rules;
- body language across peoples;
- gossip about Threefold Crossing.

Local magic exposure:

- hearth charms;
- brewing blessings;
- guest-right tokens;
- road dust wards;
- minor healing salves;
- anti-spoilage charms;
- door and threshold marks;
- contract phrases spoken over drink.

Why newborn entry works here:

The MC is physically helpless but mentally surrounded by structured input. Every day brings language, patterns, trade, recipes, social rules, and overheard world fragments.

First person likely to suspect he is unusual:

A Veyr peddler or story broker notices the baby tracking conversations too deliberately.

Built-in tensions:

- inns have little privacy;
- travelers bring danger and disease;
- local conservatives judge the household for dealing with outsiders;
- debts and guest-right obligations can entangle the family;
- the MC hears things no child should be able to contextualize.

Strengths:

- Best match for Inner Workshop data gathering.
- Strong fit for brewing/mead, language capture, trade, and social observation.
- Natural reason for outsiders to appear.

Risks:

- May make the MC's early information access too convenient unless travel is seasonal and filtered through local scale.

## Option 2: Hearthfolk Farm With Ashenkin Craft Debt

Working household name:

**Cinder-Oak Holding**

Location:

Edge of Briar Glen, near orchards, hives, and charcoal coppice routes.

Family role:

The family owns or leases a modest farm with orchards, bees, goats/sheep, and seasonal brewing. They owe an old debt or favor to a nearby Ashenkin craft family.

Household economy:

- apples/pears;
- honey;
- wax;
- small mead batches;
- wool/goat milk;
- firewood;
- charcoal labor;
- seasonal field work;
- preserved fruit;
- occasional market trips.

Parents/caretakers:

- **Mother candidate:** orchard/hive manager with practical authority in the household.
- **Father candidate:** farmer, carter, militia part-timer, or seasonal charcoal hauler.
- **Ashenkin tie:** an Ashenkin smith/kiln family saved the farm, repaired a ruined oven, guaranteed a debt, or shares old guest-right.
- **Caregiver/nurse candidate:** older village woman or poorer kin hired after childbirth complications.

Early data streams:

- bees;
- seasons;
- weather signs;
- animal care;
- orchard pruning;
- honey processing;
- mead fermentation;
- family disputes;
- Ashenkin craft visits;
- market-day preparation.

Local magic exposure:

- hive blessings;
- orchard charms;
- weather prayers;
- child-protection marks;
- fermentation rites;
- smoke and heat practices;
- Ashenkin repair marks on tools.

Why newborn entry works here:

The MC has fewer outsider data streams but deeper embodied exposure to living systems, cycles, craft patience, and local dependency.

First person likely to suspect he is unusual:

The mother, because the MC calms at strange times, watches too closely, and reacts to repeated phrases before he should understand them.

Built-in tensions:

- farm economics are fragile;
- old debt binds the family to Ashenkin outsiders;
- bad harvests matter;
- bees and brewing create useful but small-scale power;
- the MC's weirdness cannot hide behind traveler bustle.

Strengths:

- Best for bees, brewing, husbandry, seasons, and grounded childhood.
- Strong emotional family focus.
- Good contrast when he later reaches Threefold Crossing.

Risks:

- Slower access to languages, travelers, and broader System observations.

## Option 3: Shrine-House Threshold Family

Working household name:

**The White Step**

Location:

Small road shrine between Briar Glen and Threefold Crossing.

Family role:

The family tends a threshold shrine associated with safe passage, birth, death, guest-right, roads, and oaths. It is not a major temple; it is a local sacred service point.

Household economy:

- offerings;
- shrine lodging;
- funeral/birth rites;
- oath witnessing;
- road blessings;
- candle and wax use;
- copied prayers;
- minor record keeping;
- festival duties.

Parents/caretakers:

- **Mother candidate:** shrine tender, lay priestess, candle maker, or healer's assistant.
- **Father candidate:** oath witness, road warden, scribe, gardener, or former caravan guard.
- **Ashenkin tie:** maintains the shrine stones, brazier, and threshold marks.
- **Veyr tie:** route-families respect the shrine as a neutral witness point.

Early data streams:

- prayers;
- death rites;
- birth rites;
- oath language;
- traveler confessions;
- funeral customs;
- candles/wax;
- shrine inscriptions;
- religious arguments;
- road stories.

Local magic exposure:

- threshold rites;
- oath marks;
- candle charms;
- protective circles;
- funeral phrases;
- birth blessings;
- road and guest-right invocations.

Why newborn entry works here:

The MC grows up surrounded by language about thresholds, death, consent, passage, and choice, echoing the death interface without revealing it too early.

First person likely to suspect he is unusual:

A shrine elder or oath witness notices that the child reacts to threshold language with adult-level fear or attention.

Built-in tensions:

- strong thematic resonance with the moon/death interface;
- risk of early religious pressure;
- locals may interpret the MC's strangeness as blessing, possession, omen, or shrine-mark;
- secrets overheard at shrines can be dangerous.

Strengths:

- Best thematic fit for death interface, consent, and System threshold motifs.
- Gives literacy/records earlier than other options.
- Natural access to social secrets.

Risks:

- May reveal or foreground metaphysics too early.

## Option 4: Market Provisioner Kin Network

Working household name:

**Merrit & Kin Provisioners**

Location:

Between Briar Glen and Threefold Crossing, with family members in both.

Family role:

The family supplies caravans, guild factors, and market stalls with preserved food, leather goods, honey, ale, lamp oil, rope, and basic travel kits. They are not rich merchants; they are the connective tissue beneath trade.

Household economy:

- preserved food;
- travel rations;
- rope;
- waxed cloth;
- leather straps;
- lamp oil;
- honey;
- ale/mead;
- tool repair brokerage;
- message packets;
- seasonal market stalls.

Parents/caretakers:

- **Mother candidate:** accounts keeper, recipe manager, buyer, or stall negotiator.
- **Father candidate:** carter, pack organizer, militia-road contact, or warehouse keeper.
- **Veyr tie:** regular route customers and gossip exchange.
- **Stoneborn tie:** contracts for road crews and structural work camps.
- **Ashenkin tie:** heat-work repairs and kettle/oven maintenance.

Early data streams:

- inventories;
- counting;
- ledgers;
- route maps;
- trade measures;
- supply chains;
- packaging;
- quality control;
- contract terms;
- multi-people bargaining.

Local magic exposure:

- preservation charms;
- measure marks;
- contract tokens;
- road-seal knots;
- quality appraisal;
- anti-pest wards;
- message-binding tags.

Why newborn entry works here:

The MC grows up around logistics, systems, classification, quantities, labels, storage, and trade networks. This strongly supports the programmer/System architect side.

First person likely to suspect he is unusual:

The mother/account keeper catches him anticipating inventory rhythms, calming to counting chants, or staring at ledgers with impossible focus.

Built-in tensions:

- trade families are trusted and distrusted at the same time;
- mistakes in supply can kill travelers;
- guild factors may exploit or recruit the family;
- the MC's future Lenses could naturally emerge from logistics thinking.

Strengths:

- Best fit for systems/logistics/programming pattern translation.
- Great bridge to Threefold Crossing and wider travel.
- Strong foundation for later economic influence.

Risks:

- Less intimate than farm/shrine if not grounded in household relationships.

## Recommendation

Best fit for Fealor: **Option 1 or Option 4**, possibly hybridized.

Recommended hybrid:

The MC is born into a road-inn/provisioning household in or near Briar Glen. The family brews, keeps limited hives, stores traveler goods, and maintains ties to Ashenkin repair workers, Veyr route customers, and Stoneborn road crews.

This gives:

- newborn dependency;
- household intimacy;
- brewing/honey hooks;
- traveler data;
- ledgers and logistics;
- multiple peoples without city-scale overload;
- a reason for outsiders to appear;
- strong future path toward Lenses, trade, and information power.

## Candidate Canon Direction

If approved, the household should be:

- socially useful but not powerful;
- respected locally but vulnerable to debt, taxes, and road politics;
- close enough to outsiders to be watched by conservatives;
- practical rather than scholarly;
- full of repeated processes the MC can index as an infant.

## First Suspicion Sequence

Candidate early arc:

1. The MC is born into the road-inn/provisioning household.
2. Infancy is dominated by dependency, overheard language, and System-interface work.
3. A recurring Veyr peddler notices abnormal attention.
4. An Ashenkin repair visit exposes the MC to heat/craft/rune marks.
5. A Stoneborn road auditor introduces structural resonance.
6. A culturally inept adult-arrival outsider creates the first clear lesson about why birth-path acclimation matters.

## Provocateur Pass

- Assumptions to confirm:
  - The MC benefits from early access to travelers and trade information.
  - Household should support multiple creator skill translations without feeling engineered.
  - Family should be useful but not elite.
- Possible conflicts:
  - Too much traveler access can flatten the starter-region reveal curve.
  - Too many skill hooks in one household may feel convenient.
  - A road-inn setting needs strong privacy/dependency costs to preserve newborn-entry consequences.
- Missing decisions:
  - Is the household inn-first, provisioner-first, brewery-first, or shrine-adjacent?
  - Is the MC's mother the main operator, caregiver, brewer, accountant, or shrine-linked figure?
  - What is the family's biggest vulnerability: debt, tax, sickness, road danger, guild pressure, social suspicion, or noble claim?
  - Who is the caregiver/nursemaid and what social category does she occupy?
- Drift risks:
  - Turning the family into a perfect platform for the MC.
  - Overloading infancy with too much world data.
  - Making parents too generic.
- Useful pressure tests:
  - What household chore/process becomes the MC's first internal model for System routines?
  - What adult conversation does he understand too early?
  - What family problem can he perceive but not act on because he is an infant?
