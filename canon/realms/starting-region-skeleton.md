# Starting Region Skeleton

Status: candidate canon
Provenance: generated from selected hybrid of Hearthvale and Threefold Crossroads options.

## Core Shape

The MC begins in a locally simple but regionally connected place.

His home is a village-scale community where one dominant people and one close cousin people form the everyday baseline. A nearby crossroads market town exposes him to wider peoples, trade, guilds, soldiers, mercenaries, bards, peddlers, and rare outsiders.

This gives the newborn/childhood arc:

- intimacy;
- routine;
- local social pressure;
- enough isolation for weirdness to matter;
- enough outside traffic for the Inner Workshop to collect data;
- a believable ramp from village to market town to region to world.

## Working Names

All names are candidates.

- Region: **The Hearthroad Vale**
- Home village: **Briar Glen**
- Nearby market town: **Threefold Crossing**
- Main road: **The Hearthroad**
- Hill/forge district: **Ashenfold**
- Seasonal market: **The Turning Market**
- Local river/stream: **The Glenwash**

## Geography

The Hearthroad Vale is a temperate valley region where low hills, orchards, grain fields, coppiced woods, and small iron-rich ridges meet a trade road.

Key features:

- soft valley farmland;
- wooded slopes;
- clay banks;
- charcoal coppices;
- small iron and copper workings;
- orchard belts;
- apiary meadows;
- old road shrines;
- a shallow river or strong stream;
- ruins or old stones in the hills;
- one larger crossroads market within a day's cart travel.

The region is not remote wilderness. It is a lived-in, worked-over place with old claims, old roads, and new opportunities.

## Settlement Structure

### Briar Glen

Role:

The MC's likely birthplace or childhood home.

Scale:

Village or large hamlet.

Everyday peoples:

- Hearthfolk humans as the dominant local baseline.
- Ashenkin families as a close cousin people integrated through forge, kiln, charcoal, masonry, and road work.

Economic base:

- grain;
- orchards;
- goats/sheep;
- bees;
- small brewing;
- charcoal;
- pottery clay;
- repair smithing;
- road provisioning;
- seasonal labor.

Social feel:

- kinship-heavy;
- reputation-sensitive;
- practical;
- suspicious of flashiness;
- locally pious;
- proud of competence;
- quietly dependent on Ashenkin craft.

Why it works:

The MC can grow up surrounded by household rhythms, caregivers, craft, animals, brewing, bees, simple temple rites, and road rumors. This supports the newborn-entry arc without making the first years too cosmopolitan.

### Threefold Crossing

Role:

Nearby market town and regional complexity gateway.

Scale:

Small town / market hub.

Everyday peoples:

- Vale Families / Hearthfolk humans;
- Ashenkin from nearby forge/kiln/stone communities;
- Stoneborn work crews, road wardens, masons, vault keepers, and mining factors;
- Veyr route-families and seasonal traders.

Economic base:

- road tolls;
- inns;
- wagon repair;
- smithing;
- stonework;
- grain exchange;
- honey/mead trade;
- clay and pottery;
- message carrying;
- contract witnessing;
- adventurer provisioning;
- minor guild offices.

Social feel:

- busier, sharper, and more transactional than Briar Glen;
- everyone knows route, debt, kin, oath, and craft reputations;
- outsider-friendly only by local standards;
- rich in gossip and hidden rules.

Why it works:

The MC can visit rarely as a child, then more often as he ages. Each trip gives him new language, species, class, guild, trade, and System observations.

## Peoples

### Hearthfolk Humans

Status:

Candidate dominant local human culture.

Role:

Farmers, brewers, innkeepers, local priests, militia, scribes, carters, and household craft families.

Values:

- land;
- kin;
- seasonal duty;
- competence;
- guest-right;
- debt memory;
- practical piety.

Tensions:

- distrust of too much outsider influence;
- dependence on nonhuman craft and trade;
- fear of guilds or nobles taking local autonomy;
- gossip pressure around strange children.

### Ashenkin

Status:

Candidate close cousin people.

Role:

Integrated near-humans associated with heat, kiln, forge, charcoal, masonry, road work, and endurance labor.

Traits:

- heat tolerant;
- ember/mineral visual cues;
- strong hands;
- steady breathing around smoke and kiln heat;
- cultural patience around long processes;
- reputation for stubbornness and craft pride.

Social position:

Locally essential but not fully normalized by conservative Hearthfolk.

Story use:

Ashenkin give the MC early exposure to craft, controlled heat, practical magic, social otherness, and the idea that a people can be close enough to be kin but different enough to be marked.

### Stoneborn

Status:

Candidate regional people, more common at Threefold Crossing than Briar Glen.

Role:

Road builders, masons, miners, foundation warders, vault keepers, structural enchanters, and debt-ledger witnesses.

Traits:

- compact/dense build;
- endurance;
- stone resonance traditions;
- strong oath and foundation metaphors;
- long memory for debt, contract, and structural failure.

Avoid:

Do not default them into standard dwarves. Their identity should be built around weight, foundation, resonance, and obligation rather than generic mining-beard tropes.

Story use:

Stoneborn traditions can teach the MC about structure, resonance, craft logic, and the difference between System labels and local metaphors.

### Veyr

Status:

Candidate mobile trade people.

Role:

Peddlers, bards, messengers, appraisers, rumor brokers, caravan diplomats, knife-workers, route-keepers, and matchmakers.

Traits:

- sharp social memory;
- route-based identity;
- performance law;
- flexible truth customs;
- strong attention to hospitality, insult, and bargain wording;
- visible but not fully trusted.

Story use:

Veyr expose the MC to language variation, story, rumor, trade networks, information as power, and the idea that "home" can be a route rather than land.

## Travelers And Outsiders

Regularly possible:

- peddlers;
- merchants;
- bards;
- mercenaries;
- caravan guards;
- temple mendicants;
- healers;
- guild factors;
- soldiers;
- adventuring parties;
- scholars;
- monster-part buyers;
- rare species envoys with handlers.

Outsider use:

Outsiders should be events in Briar Glen and traffic in Threefold Crossing.

The first few outsider encounters should teach the MC:

- local people are not the whole world;
- species compatibility is broader than the village baseline;
- adults can be culturally inept if they arrive badly;
- System designators and local social categories do not always match;
- trade carries information as much as goods.

## Local Power Structure

Candidate layers:

- household heads;
- village reeve or elder circle;
- Hearthroad toll office;
- temple/shrine keeper;
- Ashenkin craft families;
- Stoneborn road/foundation wardens;
- Veyr route witnesses;
- militia captain;
- town contract hall;
- minor guild factors;
- distant noble or charter authority.

Power should be practical, overlapping, and occasionally contradictory.

Examples:

- Hearthfolk elders control village reputation.
- Ashenkin control essential repair and heat-work.
- Stoneborn control road safety and structural certification.
- Veyr control information and route trust.
- Temple figures control rites, legitimacy, and moral pressure.
- Guild factors control access to training, tools, and outside markets.

## Local Magic

Local magic should feel practical before it feels grand.

Common forms:

- hearth charms;
- field blessings;
- road wards;
- craft heat control;
- kiln and forge rites;
- oath marks;
- contract witnessing;
- minor healing;
- message charms;
- appraisal tricks;
- bee/honey/fermentation folk practices;
- child-protection charms;
- weather prayers that may or may not work.

Specialist forms:

- Stoneborn resonance work;
- Ashenkin heat and structural craft;
- Veyr route memory and performance-law magic;
- temple threshold rites;
- early runework on tools, doors, roads, and contracts.

This region should not start with grand battle magic. It should start with magic as a thing people use, misunderstand, inherit, regulate, and argue over.

## MC Family Placement Options

### Option A: Hearthfolk Household With Ashenkin Ties

The MC is born into a human Hearthfolk family with a close Ashenkin relative, neighbor, patron, wet nurse, craft teacher, or family debt.

Strengths:

- easiest reader entry;
- lets the MC be locally normal but near otherness;
- good for slow exposure.

### Option B: Mixed Hearthfolk / Ashenkin Line

The MC is born into a mixed or socially liminal family.

Strengths:

- stronger identity tension;
- early body/culture questions;
- good for System compatibility and species boundary themes.

Risks:

- more complexity during infancy.

### Option C: Road-Inn Family

The MC's family runs or serves a road inn, stable, shrine-house, or market support business.

Strengths:

- perfect for overheard languages, travelers, gossip, and Inner Workshop data capture;
- still locally grounded;
- gives reason for outsiders to appear.

Risks:

- may accelerate outside exposure too quickly.

### Option D: Temple / Shrine-Linked Household

The MC is born into or fostered near a local shrine.

Strengths:

- death-interface, limbo, threshold, gods-as-programs, and consent themes echo early;
- good for literacy, ritual, and hidden System clues.

Risks:

- may push religious/System reveals too early.

Recommended current choice:

Option C hybridized with A: a Hearthfolk family tied to a road inn, stable, brewery, or provisioning house, with Ashenkin craft ties nearby.

This gives the MC household intimacy plus traveler data.

## First Outsider Encounter Options

### The Culturally Inept Adult Arrival

A visibly capable outsider arrives in an impressive body but violates local norms constantly: wrong distance, wrong guest-right, wrong food behavior, wrong eye contact, wrong kinship language, wrong oath phrasing.

Use:

Shows why the System recommends from-birth acclimation for major body/culture changes.

### The Legal Nonhuman

A nonhumanoid participant arrives in a wheeled shrine, tank, litter, or constructed support frame with handlers.

Locals treat it as a legal person, trade principal, or guild member.

Use:

Shows that humanoid-compatible does not mean humanoid-only.

### The Veyr Story Broker

A Veyr bard/peddler notices the MC paying too much attention while still too young to be doing so.

Use:

First person to suspect the MC is unusual through observation rather than magic.

### The Stoneborn Foundation Auditor

A Stoneborn inspector evaluates a bridge, cellar, kiln, or road ward and explains structural resonance in language that later helps the MC understand spell architecture.

Use:

Seeds programming/rune/magic comprehension.

## Provocateur Pass

- Assumptions to confirm:
  - The MC should start in a village/road-inn context near a crossroads market.
  - Ashenkin, Stoneborn, and Veyr are useful starting peoples.
  - Local magic should be practical and craft/social first.
- Possible conflicts:
  - Too many early peoples can overload the newborn arc.
  - Too many MC-skill hooks can make the region feel engineered for him.
  - Road-inn family gives excellent data but may reduce isolation.
- Missing decisions:
  - Which family placement is canon?
  - What is the MC's birth people/body?
  - Is the mother Hearthfolk, Ashenkin, mixed, or from another group?
  - Who is the first non-family adult to notice the MC's abnormal attention?
  - What local religion says about death, thresholds, and the moon?
- Drift risks:
  - Ashenkin becoming generic fire dwarves.
  - Stoneborn becoming standard dwarves.
  - Veyr becoming generic traveling rogues.
  - Local magic becoming too System-explicit too early.
- Useful pressure tests:
  - What local custom does the MC misunderstand despite growing up there?
  - What outsider custom does he understand better than locals because of System observation?
  - What childhood crisis forces him to use the Inner Workshop without revealing it?
