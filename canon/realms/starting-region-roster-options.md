# Starting Region Roster Options

Status: candidate options
Provenance: generated from creator direction.

These are options for the MC's early-life region. They follow the current population-scope rule: a starter locality should be socially legible, usually with one to three resident or routinely mingling peoples, while travelers hint at the larger multiverse.

None of these are approved canon yet.

## Option 1: Hearthvale Human-Majority Town

Pitch:

A mostly human agrarian town with one close cousin people living among them and occasional travelers on the trade road. This is the cleanest newborn-entry start: coherent culture first, wider world later.

Resident peoples:

- **Humans / Hearthfolk**
  - Dominant local people.
  - Family-line, craft, and temple expectations are strong.
  - Practical, seasonal, suspicious of waste, proud of local competence.

- **Ashenkin**
  - Near-human people with faint mineral/ember traits: warm skin tones, heat tolerance, unusual eye shine, and a reputation for stubbornness.
  - Integrated as smiths, kiln workers, charcoal burners, masons, and road engineers.
  - Socially close enough to intermarry in some families, but still treated as "not quite us" by conservative locals.

Routinely seen outsiders:

- traveling peddlers;
- caravan guards;
- bards;
- temple mendicants;
- seasonal mercenaries;
- one or two nonhuman merchants each year who become local gossip.

Local economy:

- grain;
- orchards;
- sheep/goats;
- charcoal;
- iron repair;
- pottery/kilns;
- small apiaries;
- river trade or cart-road trade.

Magic texture:

- household charms;
- craft blessings;
- temple rites;
- minor healing;
- fire/forge superstition around Ashenkin work;
- one undertrained village spellwright or temple functionary.

Why the MC might be born here:

- Stable enough for a newborn survival arc.
- Enough books, tools, trade rumors, and craft processes to feed the Inner Workshop slowly.
- Ashenkin forge/craft culture creates early exposure to heat, metal, runes, and practical magic.
- Insular enough that the MC's weirdness has consequences.

First outsider who widens the world:

- A scaled caravan factor arrives during a harvest market and treats the town's entire social order as quaintly primitive.
- The MC sees locals freeze, bow, bargain, and gossip afterward, learning that "person" is broader than his village has taught him.

Built-in story tensions:

- conservative human families resent Ashenkin economic indispensability;
- Ashenkin youth leave for cities where their talents are better paid;
- road taxes or bandits threaten trade;
- a culturally inept adult-arrival Earthborn could cause a scandal by failing to read basic kinship and guest-right rules.

Strengths:

- Strong newborn/childhood grounding.
- Easy to understand.
- Good for slow reveal.
- Good for practical craft, brewing, bees, smithing, and first magic.

Risks:

- May feel too conventional unless Ashenkin and System details are made distinctive.

## Option 2: Rivermeet Two-People Market Town

Pitch:

A trade town where two peoples genuinely mingle because neither can dominate the river economy alone. This gives the MC early cultural complexity without overwhelming the reader.

Resident peoples:

- **Humans / Bankborn**
  - Farmers, brewers, scribes, boat owners, innkeepers, militia, temple families.
  - Think in terms of land rights, inheritance, fields, bridges, and debt.

- **Kelran**
  - Amphibious or semi-aquatic humanoids with strong river senses, webbed hands/feet, pressure tolerance, and social norms built around currents, scent, and seasonal migration.
  - Fishers, ferrymasters, divers, salvage workers, pearl/reed harvesters, river guides, and flood-watchers.
  - Humanoid-compatible but not human-minded; eye contact, touch, food-sharing, and modesty differ sharply.

Routinely seen outsiders:

- merchant barges;
- mercenaries guarding river tolls;
- bards following festivals;
- shrine boats;
- scholars studying river ruins;
- healers buying rare reeds, fish oils, and mineral salts.

Local economy:

- river tolls;
- ferrying;
- fishing;
- brewing;
- reed paper;
- salvage;
- floodplain farming;
- clay and pottery;
- river monster bounties.

Magic texture:

- water/breath magic;
- flood charms;
- boat-blessings;
- pressure and current sensing;
- river-spirit contracts or river-System interface traditions;
- early relevance for scuba/kayaking prior-life translation.

Why the MC might be born here:

- Strong fit for watercraft, breath discipline, fishing, brewing, trade, language capture, and cultural observation.
- Two resident peoples give him rich data on how System-compatible societies still differ deeply.
- River trade brings outsiders without making the town a cosmopolitan city.

First outsider who widens the world:

- A mercenary company with three visible species arrives to hunt something upriver.
- One member is clearly culturally inept in their own selected body, creating a cautionary example of ignoring birth-path acclimation.

Built-in story tensions:

- land law versus river custom;
- human inheritance versus Kelran seasonal belonging;
- flood-control politics;
- mixed families whose children do not fit either custom neatly;
- salvage rights over drowned ruins;
- religious disputes over whether river spirits are gods, programs, or local entities.

Strengths:

- More original than a simple farming village.
- Makes species difference concrete through senses and customs.
- Supports trade, water, brewing, salvage, and early outsider traffic.

Risks:

- Requires more early cultural explanation.
- MC's newborn/caretaker situation must be placed carefully across two cultures.

## Option 3: Threefold Crossroads Market Town

Pitch:

A small but important crossroads town where three peoples are routinely present: local farmers, craft-focused near-humans, and a mobile trade people. This gives early diversity, social friction, and System-world texture.

Resident/mingling peoples:

- **Humans / Vale Families**
  - Local landholders, farmers, brewers, militia, priests, and innkeepers.
  - Control most fields and formal town offices.

- **Stoneborn**
  - Compact, dense-bodied humanoids adapted to hills, mines, stonework, and endurance.
  - Not dwarves by default; avoid Tolkien assumptions.
  - Think in terms of weight, oath, foundation, resonance, and debt-ledgers.
  - Miners, masons, road builders, vault keepers, and structural warders.

- **Veyr**
  - A mobile, sharp-sensed trade people with strong memory traditions, performance law, and route-based identity.
  - Bards, peddlers, messengers, appraisers, knife-workers, matchmakers, rumor brokers, and caravan diplomats.
  - Some are resident for seasons; others pass through on route cycles.

Routinely seen outsiders:

- guild factors;
- adventuring parties;
- military patrols;
- temple auditors;
- monster-part buyers;
- rare nonhumanoid envoy or handler team.

Local economy:

- crossroads tolls;
- mining support;
- wagon repair;
- inns;
- brewing/mead;
- apiculture;
- stonework;
- trade brokerage;
- message carrying;
- adventurer provisioning.

Magic texture:

- road wards;
- oath magic;
- stone resonance;
- appraisal charms;
- bardic memory;
- trade contracts;
- minor illusion and message magic.

Why the MC might be born here:

- Excellent for data collection: many languages, customs, routes, rumors, and class/profession models.
- Supports programming/rune logic through Stoneborn structural traditions.
- Supports bard/performance/prompt/social-interface angles through Veyr traditions.
- Supports brewing/bees/craft and later Lens distribution through trade networks.

First outsider who widens the world:

- A nonhumanoid "person" arrives inside a wheeled life-support shrine managed by attendants.
- Locals treat the entity as a legal trade principal, not a monster or god.
- The MC realizes compatibility does not always mean shared body plan.

Built-in story tensions:

- Vale Families resent Stoneborn control over roads and foundations;
- Stoneborn distrust Veyr route-law and flexible truth customs;
- Veyr know everyone's secrets but hold little land;
- mixed contracts create legal knots;
- adventurers bring money and danger;
- adult-arrival entrants can hide poorly in a town where everyone tracks kinship, route, and debt.

Strengths:

- Most immediately rich.
- Gives many hooks for MC learning, trade, language, Lenses, and outsider glimpses.
- Strong bridge from starter locality to larger regions.

Risks:

- Higher complexity early.
- Needs careful restraint so the newborn phase does not become lore overload.

## Recommendation

Best fit for Fealor right now: **Option 2 or Option 3**.

Option 2 is the best if the story wants strong culture contrast without too many peoples at once. It also makes the MC's water/breath/fishing/scuba/kayaking background relevant in surprising ways.

Option 3 is the best if the story wants the MC to become an information and interface creature early: languages, rumors, contracts, craft, routes, and social systems everywhere.

Hybrid possibility:

Start with Option 1's stable household/farming base near Option 3's crossroads. The MC's home village is simple, but the nearby market town exposes him to Stoneborn, Veyr, traders, and rare outsiders during childhood.

## Provocateur Pass

- Assumptions to confirm:
  - The MC's starting region should be humanoid-compatible and locally limited.
  - Travelers should reveal the wider world before the MC leaves home.
  - Towns should usually have one to three resident/mingling peoples.
- Possible conflicts:
  - A complex market town may reduce the impact of later city-scale diversity.
  - A too-simple village may delay the MC's data-gathering engine.
  - Near-human peoples risk feeling generic unless grounded in senses, customs, economy, and System compatibility.
- Missing decisions:
  - Is the MC born in a village, town, market hub, estate, temple, or road inn?
  - Is his family part of the dominant people, a minority, mixed, or socially liminal?
  - Which outsider first gives him proof that the world is much bigger?
- Drift risks:
  - Accidentally recreating elves/dwarves/halflings under new names.
  - Treating species as visual skins rather than cultures with bodies, senses, and social rules.
  - Making the starter region too convenient for every MC skill.
- Useful pressure tests:
  - What local custom does an adult-arrival entrant violate in each option?
  - What childhood event gives the MC his first species-compatibility insight?
  - Which local profession first notices the MC is strange?
